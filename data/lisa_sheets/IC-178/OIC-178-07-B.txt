Contamination by a bioterrorism agent can be airborne, digestive or cutaneous, depending on the agent. A distinction is made between lethal and incapacitating agents.
{| class="wikitable"
|'''Classification of bioterrorism agents'''

'''according to the Centers for Diseases Control and Prevention (United States)''''
<br />
|-
|'''Category A: Lethal disease, highly transmissible or easily disseminated agents'''

Bacillus anthracis, Yersinia pestis, Francisella tularensis

Smallpox virus, Filovirus (Ebola, Marburg), Arenavirus (Lassa, Junin)

Clostridium botulinum toxins (Botulinum toxin)
<br />
|-
|'''Category B: Moderate morbidity and mortality, less easily disseminated agents'''

Coxiella burnetii, Brucella spp, Burkholderia mallei and pseudomallei

Alphavirus (Venezuelan, Eastern and Western equine encephalitis)

Ricin, Clostridium perfringens epsilon toxin, staphylococcal enterotoxin B

Pathogens transmitted to water by food: ''Salmonella spp., Shigella dysenteriae, E.coli O157H7, Vibrio Cholerae, Crypstoridium parvum''.
<br />
|-
|'''Category C: Emerging infectious diseases with potential use (possible production, potentially significant morbidity)'''

Nipah virus, Hantavirus<br />.
