'''Causes of long-term progressive loss of visual acuity:'''

- <u>Cataract</u> (nuclear, cortical or posterior subcapsular)

- <u>Evolved open-angle glaucoma</u> (diagnostic triad: elevated ocular tone + visual field alterations + glaucomatous excavation of the papilla) '''(often bilateral)''''

- <u>Hereditary retinal dystrophies:</u> '''(bilateral)'''

*Hereditary maculopathies: Stargart's disease (the most common), Best's disease, cone dystrophy, etc.
*Hereditary peripheral retinal dystrophies: pigmentary retinopathies (night blindness, osteoblasts in the fundus).

- <u>Syndromes known as "vitreomacular interface":</u> '''(often unilateral)''''

*Epimacular membranes
*Macular holes

- <u>Age-related macular degeneration:</u> '''(often bilateral but asymmetric)'''

*Early stage (drusen)
*Atrophic forms
*Exudative forms (macular choroidal neovessels)

- <u>Macular edema:</u>

*Diabetic maculopathy '''<u>(often bilateral)</u>''''
*Central retinal vein occlusion '''<u>(usually unilateral)</u>''''
*Post cataract surgery (Irvine-Gass syndrome, 3% of cases).
*Uveitis (posterior, more rarely anterior when chronic)
*Choroidal and intra-retinal neovessels (main cause: exudative AMD)

- <u>Toxic maculopathies:</u> '''(often bilateral)'''

*Maculopathy to synthetic antimalarials
*Maculopathy to MEK inhibitors
*Maculopathy to alkyl nitrite ("poppers")


'''Visual field alterations'''

- <u>Visual field impairment due to retinal damage:</u>

*Central or paracentral scotomas, very often associated with reduced visual acuity (e.g. AMD)
*Peripheral visual field deficits (e.g. rhegmatogenous retinal detachment, retinopathy pigmentosa)


- <u>Alteration of the visual field due to damage to the optic pathways:</u>

*Optic nerve damage:
**Retrobulbar optic neuritis in multiple sclerosis '''(often unilateral)''''
**Anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy '''(often unilateral)'''
**Toxic and metabolic optic neuropathies '''(usually bilateral)''': ethylic, medicinal (ethambutol), occupational (lead), metabolic (diabetes).
**Tumoral optic neuropathy: intra-orbital tumours (optic nerve glioma, optic nerve sheath meningioma), tumours of the anterior skull (sphenoid meningioma, etc).

<br />

*Lesions of the optic chiasm causing bitemporal hemianopia.
**Main cause: pituitary adenomas (progressive chiasmatic syndrome beginning with superior bitemporal hemianopia).
**Other causes in adults: saddle tuber meningioma, internal carotid aneurysm, craniopharyngioma.
**Other causes in children: glioma of the chiasma, which often occurs as part of Recklinghausen's disease, craniopharyngioma.

<br />

*Retrochiasmatic lesions of any aetiology:
**vascular (stroke, compressive haematoma): homonymous lateral hemianopsia or quadranopsia of abrupt onset
**tumour: hemianopia or homonymous lateral quadranopsia of progressive onset
**traumatic: homonymous lateral hemianopia or quadranopia contemporaneous with the trauma.

<br />

*Cortical blindness (most frequent cause: stroke in the vertebrobasilar territory)


''Note: whether the condition is uni- or bilateral (often asymmetric) is indicated in brackets when the condition is predominantly uni- or bilateral''.
